English[edit]

Etymology 1[edit]

From Latin līmes. Doublet of limit.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (UK) IPA(key): /ˈlʌɪmiːz/

Noun[edit]

limes (plural limites)

  1. A boundary or border, especially of the Roman Empire.
    • 2011, Norman Davies, Vanished Kingdoms, Penguin, published 2012, page 92:
      Their presence in the late fourth century on the River Main (immediately to the east of the Roman limes) is documented in Roman sources, as are their wars with the Alemanni.

Etymology 2[edit]

Inflected forms.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (UK) IPA(key): /lʌɪmz/
  • Rhymes: -aɪmz

Noun[edit]

limes

  1. plural of lime

Anagrams[edit]

  • Imels, Liems, Miles, Selim, miles, milse, misle, slime, smile

Finnish[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Learned borrowing from Latin līmes.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): /ˈlimes/, [ˈlime̞s̠]
  • Rhymes: -imes
  • Syllabification(key): li‧mes

Noun[edit]

limes

  1. (mathematics) limit, used mainly to read out formulas.
    lim x → c {\displaystyle \lim _{x\to c}} reads: limes x lähenee c:tä.

Latin[edit]

Etymology 1[edit]

From līmus (“askew”) +‎ -es (“going”). See also līmen.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /ˈliː.mes/, [ˈlʲiːmɛs̠]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /ˈli.mes/, [ˈliːmes]

Noun[edit]

līmes m (genitive līmitis); third declension

  1. limit, border, path.
    Synonyms: fīnis, modus, cacūmen
