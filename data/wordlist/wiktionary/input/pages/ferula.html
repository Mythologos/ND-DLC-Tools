English[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Borrowed from Latin ferula (“giant fennel”), whose stalks were once used in punishing schoolboys. Doublet of ferule.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): /ˈfɛɹələ/

Noun[edit]

ferula (plural ferulas or ferulae)

  1. (obsolete) A ferule.
    • 1613–1614, William Shakespeare, John Fletcher, “The Two Noble Kinsmen.”, in Comedies and Tragedies […], London: […] Humphrey Robinson, […], and for Humphrey Moseley […], published 1679, →OCLC, (please specify the act number in uppercase Roman numerals, and the scene number in lowercase Roman numerals):
      He humbles with a ferula the tall ones
  2. (archaic) A stroke from a cane.
    • 1916 December 29, James Joyce, chapter I, in A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man, New York, N.Y.: B[enjamin] W. Huebsch, →OCLC, page 46:
      And Old Barrett has a new way of twisting the note so that you can't open it and fold it again to see how many ferulæ you are to get.
  3. (obsolete) The imperial sceptre in the Byzantine Empire.

Translations[edit]

stroke from a cane
  • French: coup de cane m

Anagrams[edit]

  • Laufer, earful

Latin[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Uncertain but perhaps connected to festūca (“stalk, straw”).

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /ˈfe.ru.la/, [ˈfɛrʊɫ̪ä]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /ˈfe.ru.la/, [ˈfɛːrulä]

Noun[edit]

ferula f (genitive ferulae); first declension

  1. giant fennel (Ferula communis)
  2. a staff, stick, rod
  3. a splint
  4. the unramified horn of a young stag
