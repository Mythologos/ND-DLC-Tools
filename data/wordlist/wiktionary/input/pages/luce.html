English[edit]

Etymology[edit]

From Old French lus, luis, from Latin lūcius.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (UK) IPA(key): /luːs/
    • Audio (Southern England)(file)

Homophone: loose

  • Rhymes: -uːs

Noun[edit]

luce (plural luces)

  1. The pike, Esox lucius, when fully grown.
    • 1603, Michel de Montaigne, chapter 12, in John Florio, transl., The Essayes […], book II, London: […] Val[entine] Simmes for Edward Blount […], →OCLC:
      As wee hunt after beasts, so Tygers and Lyons hunt after men, and have a like exercise one upon another: Hounds over the Hare; the Pike or Luce over the Tench […].

Translations[edit]

Esox lucius
  • Bulgarian: щука f (štuka)

Etymology 1[edit]

Italian Wikipedia has an article on:
luce
Wikipedia it

From Latin lūcem (“light”), from Proto-Italic *louks, from the Proto-Indo-European root *lewk- (“bright; white”). Doublet of the borrowed lux.

Noun[edit]

luce f (plural luci)

  1. light (visible electromagnetic wave; electrical device providing light)
  2. clearance
  3. span (engineering)
