English[edit]

Etymology[edit]

From Latin apud (“at, by, in the presence of, in the writings of”).

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): /ˈæpəd/
    • Audio (Southern England)(file)
  • Audio (US)(file)

Preposition[edit]

apud

  1. Used in scholarly works to cite a reference at second hand
    Jones apud Smith means that the original source is Jones, but that the author is relying on Smith for that reference.

Translations[edit]

used in scholarly works to cite a reference at second hand
  • Armenian: առ (hy) (aṙ)
  • Catalan: apud
  • Portuguese: apud (pt)
  • Russian: у (ru) (u)

Esperanto[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Borrowed from Latin apud.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • Audio(file)
    • IPA(key): [ˈapud]
    • Rhymes: -apud
    • Hyphenation: a‧pud
  • Hyphenation: a‧pud

Preposition[edit]

apud

  1. near
    • 1910, L. L. Zamenhof, Proverbaro Esperanta‎[1]:
      Apud propra domo ŝtelisto ne ŝtelas.
      A thief doesn't steal near their own house.
  2. next to, beside, alongside, adjacent to
    • 1910, L. L. Zamenhof, Proverbaro Esperanta‎[2]:
      Apud plena manĝotablo ĉiu estas tre afabla.
      Next to a full table of food, everyone is very friendly.

Ido[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Borrowed from Esperanto apud, from Latin apud.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): /ˈa.pud/

Preposition[edit]

apud

  1. next to, beside, by, immediate vicinity
    La glaso es apud la krucho.
    The glass is next to the picher.

Synonyms[edit]

  • an (“at, on (indicates contiguity, juxtaposition)”)
  • che (“at, in, to”)

Antonyms[edit]

  • for (“far from, away from”)

Derived terms[edit]

  • apuda (“adjacent, near, neighboring”)
  • apude (“adjacently”)

Latin[edit]

Alternative forms[edit]

  • apor, aput

Etymology[edit]

Seems connected with ob and ad, thus its strict meaning would be “on to”, “unto”.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /ˈa.pud/, [ˈäpʊd̪]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /ˈa.pud/, [ˈäːpud̪]

Preposition[edit]

apud (+ accusative)

  1. at, by, near, among
    • Late 4th century, Jerome [et al.], transl., edited by Roger Gryson, Biblia Sacra: Iuxta Vulgatam Versionem (Vulgate), 5th edition, Stuttgart: Deutsche Bibelgesellschaft, published 2007, →ISBN, 1:1:
      In prīncipiō erat Verbum et Verbum erat apud Deum et Deus erat Verbum.
      In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was by God, and God was the Word.
    1. at the house or residence of; chez
  2. before, in the presence of, in the writings of, in view of
    • 68 BCE – 44 BCE, Cicero, Epistulae ad Atticum V.III.2:
      apud Lentulum ponam te in gratia.
      I will put you in favor of Lentulus.
    • c. 77 CE – 79 CE, Pliny the Elder, Naturalis Historia :
      libros … natos apud me
      [these] books completed in the writings of myself
