Latin[edit]

Etymology[edit]

From Proto-Italic *frūgjōr, from Proto-Indo-European *bʰruHg-ye-ti, from Proto-Indo-European *bʰruHg- (“to use, enjoy”). Akin to Proto-Germanic *brūkaną (“to make use of, consume”), whence English brook (Etymology 1).

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /ˈfru.or/, [ˈfruɔr]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /ˈfru.or/, [ˈfruːor]

Verb[edit]

fruor (present infinitive fruī, perfect active frūctus sum); third conjugation, deponent

  1. to enjoy; to derive pleasure from
    Synonyms: exhilarō, exsultō, dēlectō, grātulor, congrātulor, gaudeō
    Antonym: displiceō
  2. to engage (in)

Usage notes[edit]

  • The verb fruor and others like it, ūtor, fungor, potior, vescor, and their compounds, regularly govern the ablative case.

Conjugation[edit]

  • The future participle is fruitūrus.
  • A later past participle form is fruitus.
