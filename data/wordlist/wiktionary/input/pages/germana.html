Catalan[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Derived from Latin germanus (brother).

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): (Central, Balearic) [ʒərˈma.nə]
  • IPA(key): (Valencian) [d͡ʒeɾˈma.na]
  • Audio(file)

Noun[edit]

germana f (plural germanes)

  1. sister

Related terms[edit]

  • germà

Esperanto[edit]

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): [ɡerˈmana]
  • Audio:
    (file)
  • Rhymes: -ana
  • Hyphenation: ger‧ma‧na

Adjective[edit]

germana (accusative singular germanan, plural germanaj, accusative plural germanajn)

  1. German (of or pertaining to Germany, the German people, or the German language)
    Hypernym: eŭropa
  2. (la germana) Clipping of la germana lingvo (“the German language”).

Derived terms[edit]

  • germana lingvo (“German language”)
  • germanlingvano, germanparolanto (“a German speaker”)
  • germanparola, germanparolanta (“German-speaking”)
 

Related terms[edit]

  • germane (“in German”)
  • Germanio (“Germany”)
  • germano (“a German”)
 

See also[edit]

  • ĝermana (“Teutonic”)

Anagrams[edit]

  • magnare, magnerà, manager, mangerà

Latin[edit]

Pronunciation[edit]

  • germāna: (Classical) IPA(key): /ɡerˈmaː.na/, [ɡɛrˈmäːnä]
  • germāna: (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /d͡ʒerˈma.na/, [d͡ʒerˈmäːnä]
  • germānā: (Classical) IPA(key): /ɡerˈmaː.naː/, [ɡɛrˈmäːnäː]
  • germānā: (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /d͡ʒerˈma.na/, [d͡ʒerˈmäːnä]

Adjective[edit]

germāna

  1. inflection of germānus:
    1. feminine nominative/vocative singular
    2. neuter nominative/accusative/vocative plural

Adjective[edit]

germānā

  1. feminine ablative singular of germānus

Noun[edit]

germāna f (genitive germānae, masculine germānus); first declension

  1. sister
    Synonym: soror
    • 29 BCE – 19 BCE, Virgil, Aeneid 4.675:
      “Hoc illud, germāna, fuit?”
      “Was this, my sister, that [result which you wanted]?”
      (Anna speaks to Dido, who is near death from a self-inflicted wound.)
