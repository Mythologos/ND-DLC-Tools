English[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Latin , ablative of factum (“deed, fact”).

Adverb[edit]

facto (not comparable)

  1. (law) in fact; by the act or fact

Related terms[edit]

  • de facto
  • ex post facto
  • ipso facto

Latin[edit]

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /ˈfak.toː/, [ˈfäkt̪oː]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /ˈfak.to/, [ˈfäkt̪o]

Etymology 1[edit]

From faciō +‎ -tō.

Verb[edit]

factō (present infinitive factāre, perfect active factāvī, supine factātum); first conjugation

  1. to make, do, or perform regularly or frequently

Noun[edit]

factō n

  1. dative/ablative singular of factum (“fact, deed, act, doing”)

Participle[edit]

factō

  1. dative/ablative masculine/neuter singular of factus

Portuguese[edit]

Alternative forms[edit]

  • fato (Brazil)

Etymology[edit]

Learned borrowing from Latin factum. Doublet of feito.

Pronunciation[edit]

 
  • (Brazil) IPA(key): /ˈfak.tu/
  • (Brazil) IPA(key): /ˈfak.tu/
    • (Southern Brazil) IPA(key): /ˈfak.to/
  • (Portugal) IPA(key): /ˈfa.ktu/

  • Rhymes: -aktu
  • Hyphenation: fac‧to

Noun[edit]

facto m (plural factos) (European Portuguese)

  1. fact (something which is real)

Derived terms[edit]

  • de facto

Related terms[edit]

  • factício
  • factível
  • factoide
  • factual
  • fazer
  • feito
 

Spanish[edit]

Etymology[edit]

Borrowed from Latin factum. Compare the inherited doublet hecho.

Pronunciation[edit]

  • IPA(key): /ˈfaɡto/ [ˈfaɣ̞.t̪o]
  • Rhymes: -aɡto
  • Syllabification: fac‧to

Noun[edit]

facto m (plural factos)

  1. (archaic) fact (something which is real)
