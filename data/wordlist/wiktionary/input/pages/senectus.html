Latin[edit]

Etymology 1[edit]

From senex (“old”) +‎ -tus (adjective-forming suffix).

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /seˈnek.tus/, [s̠ɛˈnɛkt̪ʊs̠]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /seˈnek.tus/, [seˈnɛkt̪us]

Adjective[edit]

senectus (feminine senecta, neuter senectum); first/second-declension adjective

  1. aged, very old

  • senecta

Etymology 2[edit]

From senex (“old”) +‎ -tūs (abstract noun-forming suffix).

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /seˈnek.tuːs/, [s̠ɛˈnɛkt̪uːs̠]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /seˈnek.tus/, [seˈnɛkt̪us]

Noun[edit]

senectūs f (genitive senectūtis); third declension

  1. old age, senility
    Synonym: senecta
    Antonyms: iuventās, iuventa, iuventūs
    • 1781, C. W. Kindleben, Gaudeamus igitur:
      Post molestam senectutem
      "After a troubling old age"
