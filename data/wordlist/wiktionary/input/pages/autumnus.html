Latin[edit]

Alternative forms[edit]

  • auctumnus, autumna

Etymology[edit]

From earlier auctumnus, of unclear ultimate origin:

  • From Proto-Indo-European *h₃ewǵ- (“cold”) (compare Old Irish úacht, Lithuanian áušti (“to cool off”), Old Armenian ոյծ (oyc)).
  • Borrowed from an Etruscan word, like Etruscan 𐌀𐌕𐌖𐌍𐌄 (atune, “autumn”).[1][2]
  • From Proto-Indo-European *h₂sews- (“dry”), as in "drying up season."[3]

Possibly influenced by auctus (“enriched, enlarged, ample”).

Pronunciation[edit]

  • (Classical) IPA(key): /au̯ˈtum.nus/, [äu̯ˈt̪ʊmnʊs̠]
  • (modern Italianate Ecclesiastical) IPA(key): /au̯ˈtum.nus/, [äu̯ˈt̪umnus]

Noun[edit]

autumnus m (genitive autumnī); second declension

  1. autumn, fall

Adjective[edit]

autumnus (feminine autumna, neuter autumnum); first/second-declension adjective

  1. (relational) autumn; autumnal

Descendants[edit]

Several are learned.

  • Balkan Romance:
    • Aromanian: toamnã, tomne
    • Romanian: toamnă
  • Dalmatian:
    • autun
  • Italo-Romance:
    • Italian: autunno
    • Sicilian: autunnu, otunnu
  • North Italian:
    • Friulian: autun, atom
    • Piedmontese: auton, oton, otont, autun, autugn, oteugn, otugn
    • Romansch: atun, utuon
    • Venetian: utuno, otuno
  • Gallo-Romance:
    • French: automne
  • Occitano-Romance:
    • Catalan: autumne (literary, see tardor)
    • Occitan: auton, autona; automne (Lengadocian)
  • Ibero-Romance:
    • Asturian: otoñu
    • Galician: outono
    • Portuguese: outono
    • Spanish: otoño
  • Insular Romance:
    • Sardinian: atonnu, atognu, atongiu, atonzu
  • Borrowings:
    • → English: autumn
    • → Etruscan: 𐌀𐌕𐌖𐌍𐌄 (atune)
